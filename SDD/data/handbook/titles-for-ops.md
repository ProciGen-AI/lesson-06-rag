# Titles for Ops

## What Ops Means Here

Operations at NovaOps includes the people who make the company run: HR, IT, Finance, Sales Operations, Customer Success Operations, and cross-functional program work. Ops work is often quiet until it fails, so our titles are designed to recognize prevention, clarity, judgment, and repeatable systems.

Ops titles are used across departments with local examples. A Sales Operations Specialist may spend more time in Salesforce; an IT Operations Specialist may spend more time in Okta, Slack, or the laptop fleet; an HR Operations Specialist may spend more time in BambooHR. A Finance Operations Specialist may spend more time in billing review, approvals, reimbursement queues, or vendor renewal tracking. A Customer Success Operations employee may spend more time in Salesforce, SupportDesk, Webex seat routing, renewal health views, and customer handoff data. A Program Operations employee may spend more time in Jira, Webex, written decision records, launch calendars, and cross-functional dependency tracking. The level expectations are the same even when the tools differ.

The ops ladder is not a prize for having the most urgent Slack mentions. It is a way to name the scope and reliability of operational judgment. Strong operations work makes the right action easier for everyone else. It catches a missing approval before payroll is blocked, rejects an overbroad GitHub request before source code exposure, clarifies a stale manager record before access goes to the wrong approver, or turns a recurring Salesforce cleanup issue into a durable field rule and enablement note.

Ops employees usually work in systems that contain sensitive records. Okta controls login and MFA. BambooHR contains employee records and HR self-service. Salesforce contains customer and revenue information. Webex and Slack support collaboration. GitHub contains source code. Jira tracks planned work and cross-team delivery. AWS contains production and internal cloud resources. Billing and vendor review systems contain cost, contract, and renewal data. The more senior the ops work, the more important it is to understand not only the tool, but the business decision, data sensitivity, approval path, and handoff risk behind the tool.

Operations at NovaOps is remote-first work. A clean process should work across Israel, Germany, the United Kingdom, and the United States without requiring hallway knowledge or one person's memory. Good ops work has an owner, a written trail, a clear approval path, a review date when needed, and a backup path when the normal owner is on leave.

## How Ops Titles Are Used

The O1 through O5 ladder applies to operations work even when the local job title uses different words. HR Coordinator, HR Generalist, People Operations Lead, IT Support Specialist, Systems Administrator, Finance Controller, Sales Operations Specialist, Customer Success Operations Specialist, Program Operations Lead, and Operations Manager may all use this ladder when the work being evaluated is operational work. The ladder level comes from scope, judgment, independence, and durable impact, not from the noun in the job title.

Some roles sit partly on another ladder. A Customer Success Manager whose main work is customer relationship ownership should use the support and customer success ladder. A software engineer building internal tools should usually use the programmer ladder. A Product Manager running a launch calendar may use program operations examples for a stretch assignment, but that does not automatically move the person onto the ops ladder. When the ladder choice is debatable, the manager should explain which work is being evaluated and why. See [making a career](making-a-career.md) for promotion packet expectations.

Ops titles also do not grant access by themselves. Okta admin, BambooHR administrator, Salesforce admin, GitHub organization admin, AWS admin, Jira project admin, Webex admin, billing review, and export rights are granted by business need and approval. A senior ops employee may be trusted to design an access model, but privileged access still follows [our internal systems](our-internal-systems.md).

## Level Summary

O1, Operations Coordinator: Handles defined tasks accurately, follows checklists, escalates exceptions, and keeps records tidy. An O1 builds trust by being reliable with sensitive details.

O2, Operations Specialist: Owns recurring processes such as onboarding steps, access reviews, purchase routing, renewal calendars, or reporting. An O2 can improve a process after seeing where people get stuck.

O3, Senior Operations Specialist: Leads complex workflows across departments, writes durable operating procedures, and makes good judgment calls when rules conflict. An O3 reduces operational load for managers and employees.

O4, Operations Manager or Program Operations Lead: Owns a major operational system or program, sets metrics, manages risk, and coordinates leaders around decisions. This level may include people management, but can also be senior individual contribution.

O5, Director of Operations: Sets operating strategy across functions, prepares the company for scale, and makes sure process supports business outcomes rather than becoming bureaucracy.

## O1 Operations Coordinator

An O1 handles defined operational work with care. The scope is usually a known checklist, queue, report, calendar, data cleanup, equipment step, onboarding task, renewal reminder, meeting note, or access request intake. The work may touch sensitive systems, so accuracy and escalation matter more than speed theater.

An O1 should understand the difference between completing a task and making an unauthorized decision. For example, an O1 in HR may create a BambooHR onboarding task after People Operations confirms the start date, but should not invent compensation, manager, or work location details. An O1 in IT may prepare an Okta request for a standard employee role, but should escalate privileged access such as AWS admin, GitHub organization admin, Okta admin, Salesforce admin, or BambooHR administrator rights. An O1 in Finance may check whether a receipt exists, but should escalate a purchase above USD 1,500 or a new paid tool that may exceed USD 500 per month or USD 2,500 per year.

O1 work is strongest when the employee is dependable with details that are easy to get wrong. Examples include using full names and email addresses when duplicate names exist, distinguishing Rachel Green in Sales from Rachel Stein in Customer Success, distinguishing Daniel Levi in Engineering from Daniel Cohen in Sales, and distinguishing Maya Cohen from Maya Rosen. A ticket that says "add Daniel to Salesforce" is not ready for action. A strong O1 asks for the full name, email address, department, manager, system, business reason, data visible, and expected duration.

Functional O1 examples include:

- HR: Send a preboarding checklist, confirm that BambooHR has the employee's legal name and preferred name, route a SecureSign packet, check that Maya Cohen's onboarding tasks have an owner, and escalate if manager or start-date information conflicts.
- IT: Triage a Webex or MFA ticket, verify the employee is signing in through Okta, update a laptop fleet record after a confirmed shipment, and escalate if the device will access Salesforce, GitHub, BambooHR, or AWS from an unmanaged endpoint.
- Finance: Validate that a reimbursement has a receipt, match a vendor invoice to an approved renewal record, prepare a billing review queue, and escalate if a purchase is being split to avoid review.
- Sales Operations: Create or correct Salesforce records from an approved source, route an Account Executive access request, check that a Salesforce export request names the customer and purpose, and escalate if a non-Sales or non-CS employee requests broad CRM access.
- Customer Success Operations: Confirm that a Customer Success Specialist has the right Salesforce, Webex, Slack, and SupportDesk access, update a renewal handoff checklist, and escalate if customer notes are missing or a Webex seat is blocked by license limits.
- Program Operations: Prepare a Jira project checklist, gather Webex meeting notes, update a launch dependency tracker, and escalate when a decision affects budget, access, customer commitments, or release timing.

An O1 should keep records tidy. That means updating the system of record rather than relying on memory or a private note. BambooHR is the source for HR records and completed written reviews. Jira is for planned work and delivery status. Salesforce is for customer and revenue workflows. GitHub is for code review and engineering decisions. Okta and the IT request portal are for access paths. If a Slack conversation produces a decision, the O1 should help move the conclusion into the durable system or ask the owner to do it.

Strong O1 evidence includes accurate completion of recurring tasks, fewer returned tickets for missing information, clean handoffs during onboarding or offboarding, careful escalation of sensitive requests, and visible improvement in checklist quality. Weak O1 evidence includes moving tickets quickly while leaving records unclear, asking approvers to decide from incomplete information, copying old access from another employee without checking role need, or treating a private spreadsheet as the source of truth.

## O2 Operations Specialist

An O2 owns recurring operational processes. The work still has boundaries, but the employee can run the process end to end, explain the approval path, catch common exceptions, and improve the flow after seeing where people get stuck. O2 work should reduce repeated questions for managers and employees.

An O2 should be able to describe the normal workflow and the exception workflow. For access, that means knowing that standard access begins with role need, manager approval, IT review, and Finance review when spend thresholds are crossed. For restricted systems, the O2 should know that Salesforce access is limited to Sales, Customer Success, and approved operational support roles; GitHub is limited to Engineering, QA, Product roles that need code visibility, and approved contractors; BambooHR records beyond employee self-service are limited to People Operations; and AWS access follows least privilege. For Webex, the O2 should know that customer-facing roles take priority when seats are tight and that license expansion may need IT and Finance review.

An O2 does not merely execute a checklist; they notice checklist failure. If the onboarding checklist asks for a manager approval but Noam Sharon's manager record is stale after a move from Customer Success to Product, an O2 should stop and route the approval to the current role owner rather than blindly following an outdated field. If a vendor owner in the operational database says SupportDesk belongs to IT, but the work request is for Customer Success agent coverage, an O2 should name both the system owner and business owner instead of forcing a false choice.

Functional O2 examples include:

- HR: Own the onboarding task sequence from signed offer through day 30, maintain BambooHR review reminders for February and August, track contractor paperwork in the correct workflow, and make sure manager changes are corrected before sensitive approvals route.
- IT: Own the access request queue for standard systems, maintain the quarterly access review tracker, reclaim stale Slack guest or Webex seats, and document the reason for narrowing an overbroad Okta, GitHub, or AWS request.
- Finance: Own recurring spend approvals, reimbursement exception routing, vendor renewal calendars, and billing review queues; identify when a new tool crosses USD 500 per month or USD 2,500 per year; and route equipment purchases above USD 1,500 to Tom Baker or the Finance process before purchase.
- Sales Operations: Own Salesforce user setup for Sales roles, maintain required fields for pipeline hygiene, prepare monthly win/loss reporting inputs, and catch requests that ask for export access without a clear business reason.
- Customer Success Operations: Own SupportDesk and Salesforce handoff hygiene for renewals, monitor customer-facing Webex seat pressure, maintain customer health report inputs, and coordinate with Sales Ops when account ownership or customer status is unclear.
- Program Operations: Own a launch checklist, a Jira dependency board, a recurring cross-functional review, or a quarterly planning calendar; make sure decisions have owners, dates, and review points; and keep the program from depending on a single person's memory.

O2 improvement examples are practical and close to the work. A Sales Ops O2 may change a Salesforce request form so employees must identify account, role, export need, and duration before the ticket can be submitted. An IT O2 may add a required review-date field for temporary AWS ReadOnly access. An HR O2 may update BambooHR onboarding tasks so People Operations sees missing emergency contact details before day one. A Finance O2 may add a billing-review status that distinguishes "waiting on receipt" from "waiting on Finance approval."

Strong O2 evidence includes running a recurring process without manager rescue, reducing returned or incomplete requests, improving cycle time while preserving approval quality, catching edge cases before they become incidents, and making a process understandable to a backup owner. Weak O2 evidence includes being the only person who knows the workaround, accepting every urgent request as equally urgent, or improving convenience by removing necessary review.

## O3 Senior Operations Specialist

An O3 leads complex workflows across departments. The work often has conflicting rules, unclear ownership, stale data, customer pressure, budget constraints, and sensitive information. An O3 is expected to clarify the decision, recommend a path, involve the right owners, and leave a written trail that future teams can use.

Senior operations judgment is visible when rules conflict. If a Customer Success leader asks for immediate Salesforce export access for a Product employee because a customer is upset, an O3 should separate the customer need from the permission request. The answer may be a curated customer note in Jira or Salesforce, a time-boxed report prepared by Sales Ops, or a narrower view permission, not broad export access. If an engineer asks for AWS Admin to debug a production issue, an O3 should help route least-privilege or break-glass access with a review date rather than treating seniority as approval.

An O3 writes operating procedures that survive absence. A durable procedure names purpose, owner, systems involved, approval requirements, data sensitivity, cycle time expectations, exception path, and review date. It should be findable in the appropriate internal location and referenced from Jira, BambooHR, Salesforce, or the IT request portal as needed. A procedure is not durable if only the author understands the abbreviations.

Functional O3 examples include:

- HR: Redesign the preboarding-to-day-30 workflow so People Operations, IT, managers, and Finance know which data is required by which date; clean up BambooHR manager records after transfers; and create an exception path for cross-border contractor paperwork without exposing private employee data in public spaces.
- IT: Lead a quarterly restricted-access review across Okta, GitHub, Jira, AWS, Salesforce admin, and BambooHR admin; coordinate with managers who do not respond within five business days; and document removals or downgrades so access changes are explainable.
- Finance: Lead a vendor renewal readiness workflow that combines contract notice windows, seat counts, security review, business owner input, and budget approval; call out Webex seat-limit risk or AWS billing anomalies early enough for leaders to decide.
- Sales Operations: Lead a Salesforce data hygiene program across Sales and Customer Success, define when Account Executives, Customer Success Managers, and approved ops roles can see or export customer data, and make renewal and pipeline reports trustworthy without hiding source data issues.
- Customer Success Operations: Lead a customer escalation workflow that keeps SupportDesk, Salesforce, Webex notes, and Jira handoffs consistent; coordinate with Product and Engineering when customer issues need defect review; and protect customer trust without promising engineering dates that have not been approved.
- Program Operations: Lead a cross-functional launch, compliance checkpoint, systems migration, or office-equipment rollout; keep decisions in Jira or the NovaOps wiki; and ensure stakeholders can see owner, deadline, dependency, risk, and next decision.

O3 work often reduces invisible load. Managers should not have to re-explain the same access rule every week. Customer Success should not have to chase three systems to understand whether a customer escalation was handed to Engineering. Finance should not discover a renewal inside the notice window because a contract calendar lived in someone's private notes. HR should not learn on day one that IT never received the shipping address.

Strong O3 evidence includes cross-functional processes that become easier to run, written procedures used by multiple teams, better audit readiness, fewer stale access findings, reduced renewal surprises, cleaner Salesforce or BambooHR data, fewer repeated questions, and decisions that appropriately balance speed, cost, security, customer trust, and employee privacy. Weak O3 evidence includes personally solving every exception while leaving the system unchanged, turning simple workflows into bureaucracy, or using authority to override the actual business owner.

## O4 Operations Manager or Program Operations Lead

An O4 owns a major operational system, program, or function. This level may include people management, but people management is not required. An O4 may manage an HR operations team, own IT operations and access governance, lead Finance operations for spend and renewals, own Sales and Customer Success operations systems, or run a company-critical program across departments.

O4 scope is measured by system importance, risk, and leadership required. A person can be O4 without a large team if they own a high-risk operational system and coordinate leaders around decisions. A person can manage people and still be below O4 if the work is narrow, heavily directed, and mostly transactional. The title reflects operating responsibility, not calendar size.

An O4 sets metrics. Useful ops metrics are not vanity counts. They include access request cycle time by risk level, percentage of privileged access with review dates, onboarding readiness before day one, returned request rate, stale Salesforce ownership count, renewal calendar accuracy, time to customer escalation handoff, audit findings closed, Webex or paid-seat utilization, reimbursement exception aging, and program decisions captured by the deadline. The O4 should explain what the metric is meant to improve and what tradeoff it could accidentally distort.

An O4 manages risk across functions. If Webex seats are over limit and renewal is inside the notice window, the O4 does not simply ask IT to buy more seats. They coordinate with IT, Finance, Sales, and Customer Success to decide whether to reclaim inactive seats, prioritize customer-facing roles such as Rachel Stein, expand the contract, or adjust meeting practices. If GitHub contractor access is growing, the O4 makes sure manager approval, IT approval, repository scope, and end dates are enforced for contractors such as priya.raman.contractor@novaops.example or alex.kim.contractor@novaops.example. If Salesforce access requests increase from non-revenue teams, the O4 sets a route for curated customer context instead of broad CRM access.

Functional O4 examples include:

- HR: Own People Operations systems and lifecycle workflows across preboarding, onboarding, reviews, transfers, leave coordination, and offboarding; maintain privacy boundaries; and coordinate with Finance and IT before process changes affect pay, benefits, equipment, or access.
- IT: Own access governance, endpoint readiness, laptop fleet quality, Okta and MFA operations, and restricted-system review; set least-privilege patterns; and coordinate with Engineering, Product, Sales, CS, HR, and Finance on system risk.
- Finance: Own the operational cadence for billing review, spend approvals, vendor renewals, reimbursement controls, and finance-facing system hygiene; make cost decisions visible without turning Finance into a last-minute blocker.
- Sales Operations: Own Salesforce operating quality for Sales, including role access, pipeline hygiene, territory or ownership process, forecast inputs, and handoffs to Customer Success; partner with Liam Oconnor and Sales leaders on process decisions.
- Customer Success Operations: Own renewal operations, customer health data, SupportDesk and Salesforce handoffs, customer-facing license prioritization, and escalation operating rules; partner with Yael Romano and CS leaders on retention and support quality.
- Program Operations: Own a major company program such as a systems migration, compliance readiness cycle, multi-quarter launch operating model, or cross-functional planning cadence; make sure leaders decide from shared facts.

O4 communication should reduce ambiguity for leaders. Strong O4 work names the decision needed, the options, the owner, the cost or security implication, the affected customers or employees, the deadline, and the review date. It is not enough to run a meeting. The O4 should make sure the decision exists after the meeting in Jira, the NovaOps wiki, the request system, BambooHR, Salesforce, or another appropriate durable record.

Strong O4 evidence includes a major process or system that became measurably safer, faster, cheaper, clearer, or more scalable; leaders who can rely on a shared operating cadence; fewer high-risk exceptions; audit or access reviews that close cleanly; improved customer or employee experience through better operations; and backup ownership that works when the O4 is unavailable. Weak O4 evidence includes creating dashboards no one uses, escalating every decision upward, adding approval steps without reducing risk, or being indispensable because the system remains undocumented.

## O5 Director of Operations

An O5 sets operating strategy across functions. This level is rare and requires repeated company-level impact. The work crosses HR, IT, Finance, Sales Operations, Customer Success Operations, program operations, and executive decision-making. An O5 prepares NovaOps for scale while keeping process useful rather than performative.

An O5 should see patterns that are hard to see from one function alone. For example, stale manager data in BambooHR can misroute access approvals in Okta, delay laptop shipment, confuse promotion packets, and produce the wrong Salesforce or GitHub access path. Webex seat pressure can look like an IT licensing problem, but may also reveal customer coverage needs, renewal timing, budget tradeoffs, and unclear meeting norms. Salesforce export requests can look like a Sales Ops queue issue, but may involve customer confidentiality, Product discovery practices, and Finance reporting needs.

O5 strategy should connect operations to business outcomes. Strong strategy may include a company-wide access governance model, an operating calendar that aligns performance reviews, promotion cycles, vendor renewals, access reviews, and budget planning, or a unified onboarding and offboarding control model that reduces day-one failures and stale access. It may include choosing which systems need stricter controls, which processes need self-service, and which exceptions require executive review.

An O5 is accountable for operating design, not just policy language. If a policy says that managers must respond to access reviews within five business days, the O5 makes sure the review workflow can show who has not responded, what happens next, how removals are documented, and how exceptions are audited. If Finance approval is required above spend thresholds, the O5 makes sure employees know how to submit the business reason before committing company data or money to a tool. If customer-facing roles take priority for Webex seats, the O5 ensures IT, Sales, and CS can apply that priority without private negotiation.

Company-level O5 examples include:

- Creating a unified employee lifecycle operating model from offer through offboarding, including BambooHR, Okta, laptop fleet, Slack, Webex, Salesforce, GitHub, Jira, AWS, billing, and separation access removal.
- Setting an access governance strategy that distinguishes standard access, restricted access, privileged access, contractor access, break-glass access, and temporary project access.
- Building a vendor and renewal operating model that combines security review, owner confirmation, contract notice windows, seat utilization, budget thresholds, and business continuity risk.
- Establishing a cross-functional program operations practice so major launches, audits, migrations, and customer commitments have consistent owners, written decisions, and risk reviews.
- Reducing bureaucracy by retiring approval steps that no longer reduce risk while strengthening the steps that protect employee data, customer data, source code, production systems, and company spend.

Strong O5 evidence includes durable operating strategy adopted by multiple functions, reduced company-level risk, better executive decisions because operating facts are clearer, improved employee or customer experience at scale, cleaner audits, meaningful cost control without service damage, and development of other operations leaders. Weak O5 evidence includes owning every escalation personally, producing a strategy deck that teams do not use, adding process because process feels senior, or measuring operations only by ticket volume.

## Approval Workflows and Decision Rights

Ops employees are often the people who make approvals real. The basic rule is simple: the approver should understand the business need, the risk, the cost, and the data involved. The person processing a request should not turn an incomplete request into an approval by guessing.

Most access requests should include the system name, business reason, permission level or role, expected duration, manager, whether customer, employee, source-code, billing, or infrastructure data will be visible, and whether the request creates new paid spend. A request that says "same as Rachel" or "same as the engineering team" is not enough. Use full names and NovaOps email addresses when comparing to another person, and explain the work need rather than copying all permissions.

Manager approval confirms role need. IT approval confirms security, least privilege, license impact, and managed-device requirements. Finance reviews requests that create new paid spend above USD 500 per month or USD 2,500 per year, and equipment purchases above USD 1,500. People Operations owns HR record and employment-document workflows. Sales and Customer Success leaders help review Salesforce and customer-facing system access. Engineering and IT review GitHub, AWS, Datadog, and production-adjacent access.

Privileged access needs special handling. AWS admin, GitHub organization admin, Okta admin, Salesforce admin, Jira project admin, BambooHR administrator rights, and production break-glass access require a business reason, manager approval, IT approval, and a named end date or review date. Contractor GitHub access requires explicit manager and IT approval even when the contractor already has Slack, Jira, or Okta. Slack guest accounts require a business owner and expiration date.

Finance approval is not a rubber stamp after a tool has already been adopted. Free trials can still create data retention, security, customer confidentiality, and renewal obligations. If a team wants a new tool or paid expansion, the request should identify users, expected cost after trial, data type, whether Okta SSO is available, and whether customer, employee, source-code, billing, or infrastructure data will be stored.

Strong ops judgment keeps approval paths narrow and visible. A narrow approval is not the same as a no. An engineer who asks for AWS Admin may receive AWS ReadOnly while Omar Weiss or IT confirms the production need. A Product employee who asks for Salesforce export rights may receive curated customer notes instead. A Customer Success employee blocked by Webex seat availability may receive temporary meeting coverage while IT and Finance decide whether to reclaim or expand seats.

## Operational Messiness and Edge Cases

Real operations work is messy. Records drift. People transfer. Contractors start before every system has perfect context. Vendor owners in a database may not match the team that uses the tool. Two employees may share a first name. A manager may approve the business need but forget the license cost. A Slack thread may contain the real decision while Jira still shows the old status.

Duplicate names require extra care. Use full names and email addresses for access, HR, customer, and payroll questions. Rachel Green in Sales is not Rachel Stein in Customer Success. Daniel Levi in Engineering is not Daniel Cohen in Sales. Maya Cohen is not Maya Rosen. Sara Ben-David and Sara Cohen both work in HR but may own different steps. An ops employee who notices a duplicate-name risk should stop and clarify before acting.

Stale manager or department data should be resolved through the workflow owner. Noam Sharon moved from Customer Success to Product, and some records may still show old context. Access and approval should be based on the current role and current approval chain, not stale manager data. An O1 should escalate the mismatch. An O2 should route the exception correctly. An O3 or higher should fix the root process that allowed stale data to affect approvals.

Contractors need explicit scope. A contractor may have Jira and Slack Guest access but still need separate GitHub review. Contractor access should match the statement of work, repository or project scope, manager sponsor, IT approval, and end date. Contractors should not be added to broad GitHub teams just to make one repository visible, and contractor equipment should not be passed to a replacement contractor without IT updating the asset record.

Vendor ownership can be imperfect. SupportDesk may appear under IT even though Customer Success uses agent seats. Figma ownership may appear as Engineering even though Product Design uses it heavily. Miro renewal terms may differ between a portal and an order form. These mismatches are not permission to bypass review. They are signals to name the business owner, system owner, document owner, and approver separately.

Urgency does not erase controls. Payroll blockers, production incidents, customer incidents, security exposure, and expiring renewal windows may require faster response. Even then, the accountable lead should name severity, owner, next update time, working channel, approval path, and post-incident cleanup. Temporary access granted for an emergency needs an end date or review date. A fast path without cleanup becomes stale access.

Remote-first work creates timing edge cases. A handoff across Israel, Germany, the United Kingdom, and the United States should not depend on a live Webex meeting when a written decision would work. However, live discussion is useful when the decision is ambiguous, sensitive, or likely to sprawl. Strong ops employees know when to move from Slack to Webex and then back into a durable written record.

## Program Operations

Program operations is the ops discipline for cross-functional work that does not sit cleanly inside one department. It includes launches, migrations, audit readiness, customer escalation readiness, renewal windows, onboarding waves, office-equipment rollouts, compliance checkpoints, and company operating cadences. Program ops work is evaluated on clarity, ownership, risk management, and the ability to move work across functions without creating confusion.

At O1, program ops may mean preparing agendas, taking notes, updating Jira fields, and confirming that action items have owners. At O2, it may mean owning a recurring launch checklist or quarterly planning calendar. At O3, it may mean leading a launch readiness workflow across Product, Engineering, QA, Sales, Customer Success, IT, and Finance. At O4, it may mean owning the program operating model for a major system migration or audit cycle. At O5, it may mean setting the company-wide standard for how cross-functional programs are selected, staffed, measured, and closed.

Good program ops does not create ceremonies for their own sake. A meeting is useful when it resolves a decision. A Jira board is useful when it shows owner, state, blocker, and next step. A Webex recording is useful only if the decision is summarized somewhere searchable. A launch checklist is useful when it names customer impact, access needs, billing changes, support readiness, rollback or contingency, owner, due date, and review date.

Program ops evidence should show that work became easier to coordinate, not merely that the program ops person attended every meeting. Strong evidence includes fewer missed handoffs, faster risk escalation, clearer launch readiness, reduced duplicate status reporting, better customer or employee communication, and decisions that remained findable after the program ended.

## Promotion Evidence

Strong Ops evidence includes fewer repeated questions, faster cycle times, cleaner audits, safer access, better forecasting, and processes that continue working while the owner is on vacation. The best examples show that the operator improved the system, not just personally worked harder.

Ops employees should document the before and after: what was broken, what changed, what risk was reduced, and how other teams can now move faster. See [our internal systems](our-internal-systems.md) for access and approval rules.

Good evidence is specific and recent. It should include the operating problem, the systems involved, the old failure mode, the change made, the result, and the remaining risk. Useful artifacts may include Jira issues, BambooHR workflow records, access review exports, Salesforce field-quality reports, renewal calendars, billing review notes, Webex seat utilization, vendor decision records, written procedures, launch checklists, audit closure summaries, and peer input tied to the ladder.

Evidence should map to the next level. O1 to O2 evidence shows the employee can own a recurring process, not just complete assigned steps. O2 to O3 evidence shows the employee can lead messy cross-functional workflows and improve the system. O3 to O4 evidence shows ownership of a major operational system or program with metrics, risk management, and leader coordination. O4 to O5 evidence shows company-level operating strategy adopted across functions.

Strong evidence examples include:

- HR: Reduced day-one onboarding misses by changing the BambooHR-to-IT handoff, adding required manager and shipping fields, and documenting exception paths for cross-border contractors.
- IT: Cut stale privileged access findings by adding review dates, owner reminders, and a removal path for GitHub, AWS, Okta, Jira, and Salesforce admin roles.
- Finance: Prevented renewal surprises by building a calendar that combines notice windows, owner confirmation, security review, seat counts, and spend thresholds.
- Sales Ops: Improved forecast trust by cleaning Salesforce ownership rules, documenting export permissions, and reducing duplicate or stale account fields.
- CS Ops: Reduced repeat escalations by aligning SupportDesk, Salesforce, Jira, and Webex handoff notes, with clear ownership for customer follow-up.
- Program Ops: Led a cross-functional launch readiness process that named blockers early, captured decisions in Jira or the wiki, and reduced last-week surprises.

Promotion packets should avoid vague claims. "Runs ops well" is not enough. "Reduced returned access tickets from 34% to 11% by requiring system, data type, duration, and manager approval before IT review" is evidence calibration can evaluate. If metrics are not available, use concrete artifacts and before-after examples.

## What Does Not Count as Promotion Evidence

Working late is not promotion evidence by itself. Being the person everyone pings is not promotion evidence by itself. Holding admin access is not promotion evidence by itself. A bigger calendar is not promotion evidence by itself. Being friendly, calm, or trusted is useful, but a promotion packet still needs examples of scope and impact.

Closing many easy tickets can show reliability, but it does not prove higher-level operations work if the tickets were routine, poorly documented, or created follow-up cleanup. Owning a crisis is weak evidence if the employee's process caused the crisis or if nothing changed afterward. Writing a long policy that nobody uses is weak evidence. Creating a dashboard that leaders do not consult is weak evidence. Moving work into a private spreadsheet can make one person faster while making the company more fragile.

Seniority is not a license to bypass controls. An O4 or O5 still needs approval for privileged access, paid tools, employee records, customer exports, and production-adjacent systems. Conversely, a junior employee may process sensitive work if the decision rights are clear and the review path is followed. Access level and ladder level are related only through business need, not status.

Volume can be misleading. A Sales Ops employee who creates hundreds of Salesforce updates may be doing O1 or O2 work if the updates follow a defined queue. A Sales Ops employee who redesigns the ownership rules, reduces duplicate accounts, and gives Sales and CS a shared operating model may be showing O3 or O4 work. An IT employee who grants many tickets quickly may create risk if access is too broad. An IT employee who slows one request to prevent customer data exposure may be showing stronger judgment.

Ops managers should be careful not to reward hidden heroics. If an employee is indispensable because no one else can run payroll close, understand Webex seat allocation, process contractor GitHub access, or interpret the renewal calendar, the system is too fragile. Strong senior ops work makes coverage easier.

## Title Calibration

Calibration compares evidence to the ladder, not to local title habits. A person with "Coordinator" in the job title may be operating at O2 if they own a recurring process end to end. A person with "Lead" in the job title may still need more O4 evidence if they do not own a major system or program. A department may use title words differently, so calibration should ask what the person actually owns, which decisions they make, what risk they manage, and what changed because of their work.

Managers should calibrate ops titles with People Operations and the relevant department leader. Finance should confirm compensation changes before a promotion decision is final. For cross-functional roles, include input from the teams that rely on the process. A Sales Ops packet may need Sales and Customer Success context. An IT Ops packet may need HR, Engineering, and Finance context. A Program Ops packet may need leaders from every function touched by the program.

Useful calibration questions:

- What recurring process, system, or program does the employee own?
- What decisions can they make without step-by-step direction?
- What approvals do they route, and do they understand the risk behind them?
- What evidence exists in BambooHR, Jira, Salesforce, Okta, GitHub, Webex, AWS, billing review, procedures, or audit records?
- Did the work reduce repeated questions, stale access, cycle time, spend surprises, customer confusion, employee confusion, or manager load?
- Did the employee create leverage for others, or only become personally faster?
- If the employee were unavailable for two weeks, would the process still work?
- Is the evidence repeated at the proposed level, or is it one unusual emergency?

Calibration should name edge cases directly. If someone transferred functions, separate evidence from the old role and new role. If a project was canceled, the work can still count if it showed next-level judgment before cancellation. If performance is uneven, calibration may approve a growth plan instead of a promotion. If an employee has strong technical system knowledge but avoids documentation or handoff, that may be a gap for senior ops levels.

## Retrieval-Friendly Facts

The NovaOps operations ladder has five levels: O1 Operations Coordinator, O2 Operations Specialist, O3 Senior Operations Specialist, O4 Operations Manager or Program Operations Lead, and O5 Director of Operations.

O1 operations employees handle defined tasks accurately, follow checklists, escalate exceptions, and keep records tidy. O2 operations employees own recurring processes such as onboarding steps, access reviews, purchase routing, renewal calendars, or reporting. O3 operations employees lead complex cross-functional workflows and write durable operating procedures. O4 operations employees own major operational systems or programs, set metrics, manage risk, and coordinate leaders. O5 operations employees set operating strategy across functions and prepare NovaOps for scale.

Ops work at NovaOps includes HR, IT, Finance, Sales Operations, Customer Success Operations, and cross-functional program operations. Ops employees may work in Okta, BambooHR, Salesforce, Webex, GitHub, Jira, AWS, billing review, the laptop fleet, SupportDesk, and other approved systems depending on role need.

Operations titles do not automatically grant access. Privileged access such as AWS admin, GitHub organization admin, Okta admin, Salesforce admin, Jira project admin, and BambooHR administrator rights requires a business reason, manager approval, IT approval, and a named end date or review date. Contractor GitHub access requires explicit manager and IT approval even when the contractor already has Jira or Slack.

Finance review is required for new paid spend above USD 500 per month or USD 2,500 per year, and equipment purchases above USD 1,500 require Finance review before purchase. Customer-facing Webex needs may be prioritized when seats are tight.

Promotion evidence for operations should show before and after: what was broken, what changed, what risk was reduced, and how other teams can move faster. Strong evidence includes fewer repeated questions, faster cycle times, cleaner audits, safer access, better forecasting, clearer Salesforce or BambooHR data, reduced renewal surprises, and processes that continue working while the owner is on vacation.

NovaOps does not promote operations employees merely for working longer hours, being the person everyone pings, holding admin access, closing many easy tickets, writing unused policies, attending many meetings, or owning a crisis that did not lead to a better system.
