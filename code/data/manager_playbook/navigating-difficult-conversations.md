# Navigating Difficult Conversations

## Prepare Before You Talk

Difficult conversations deserve preparation. Write down the purpose of the conversation, the facts you can support, the decision that has already been made, and the parts that are still open. If the topic involves performance risk, leave, accommodations, harassment, discrimination, compensation, or termination, involve People Operations before the conversation.

Do not use a difficult conversation to unload frustration. The employee should leave understanding what happened, what it means, and what happens next.

Preparation should include the channel, timing, and follow-up owner. Remote-first work makes it tempting to handle hard topics in Slack because it feels efficient. Do not do that for anything that could affect trust, performance, compensation, leave, role scope, or continued employment. Use Webex or an in-person conversation when available, then document the agreed facts in the appropriate system.

Before the conversation, decide whether the topic is a coaching conversation, a feedback conversation, an investigation intake, a formal performance step, or a final decision. Those are different conversations. If you are not sure which one you are having, involve People Operations before the meeting.

## Conversation Structure

Start directly and respectfully. Name the topic in the first minute. Long warmups often make the conversation more anxious. Use concrete examples and avoid character judgments. Pause for the employee's perspective, but do not pretend a final decision is still open when it is not.

For a conflict conversation, focus on behavior, impact, and future working agreements. For a performance conversation, focus on expectations, evidence, gaps, and next steps. For a compensation or promotion disappointment, explain the process and the evidence without inventing a softer reason.

A useful structure is: purpose, facts, impact, employee perspective, decision or next step, and follow-up. Purpose tells the employee why the conversation is happening. Facts keep the discussion grounded. Impact explains why the issue matters. Employee perspective makes room for missing context. The decision or next step prevents ambiguity. Follow-up gives both people a record of what happens after the call.

Avoid phrases that sound kind but blur the message. "This is not ideal" may be too vague when the actual message is "the customer renewal was put at risk." "There are concerns about fit" may be unfair when the actual issue is missed Salesforce updates, late Jira delivery, or repeated unreviewed production changes. Say the work behavior and the business impact.

## Common Conversation Types

For priority conflict, ask what work is competing, what deadline is real, and which decision is needed. Then decide or escalate. Do not ask the employee to satisfy impossible priorities quietly.

For peer conflict, focus on observable behavior and working agreements. Do not become a messenger for anonymous complaints unless People Operations is guiding an investigation. If two employees need a reset, write down the future agreement: who owns which work, what channel to use, and how disagreements will be escalated.

For disappointment about promotion or compensation, explain the process and the evidence. Do not soften a no by promising a yes next cycle. A better answer is: "The packet did not show sustained next-level scope yet. The strongest missing evidence is cross-team decision ownership. We will review that evidence again in the September cycle."

For leave, accommodations, health, or family issues, route details to People Operations. The manager can discuss coverage, schedule, and work expectations. The manager should not ask for diagnosis details or make private side agreements that conflict with leave administration.

For policy or security concerns, slow down. A GitHub access violation, accidental BambooHR visibility, Salesforce export problem, or AWS permission issue may require IT, People Operations, Legal, or Finance. Do not promise confidentiality beyond what the company can honor.

## Handling Emotion

The employee may be angry, embarrassed, silent, defensive, or relieved. Let there be a pause. You do not need to fill every silence. Acknowledge the emotion without changing the message: "I can see this is frustrating. The decision is still the same, and I want to walk through what happens next."

Do not argue point by point if the purpose is to communicate a final decision. Do not diagnose the employee's motives. Do not say "I know exactly how you feel." You can be humane without pretending the situation is something it is not.

If the employee raises a new fact that could materially change the situation, pause the meeting and contact People Operations. New facts may include approved leave, missing access, harassment claims, accommodation needs, or evidence that the manager's examples are wrong. Pausing is better than pushing through a script that may now be incomplete.

## Termination Conversations

For a final termination conversation, the manager is responsible for scheduling and delivering the conversation. People Operations should prepare the materials, attend or be immediately available when appropriate, and handle follow-up logistics and paperwork after the conversation.

Be brief, clear, and humane. State that employment is ending, name the effective date, and explain the high-level reason consistent with the approved script. Do not debate the decision, negotiate severance, or make promises about references, equipment, benefits, or access. Tell the employee that People Operations will follow up with written details.

The final conversation is not the place to re-run the entire performance history. If the employee was on a correction plan, they should already know the benchmarks and the risk. In the final meeting, use the approved reason and move to logistics. If the employee asks for severance details, benefits, equipment exceptions, or final pay timing, hand that to People Operations.

Schedule access removal before the meeting, but do it with care. IT should know the timing for Okta, Slack, GitHub, AWS, Salesforce, BambooHR, and device management. For most involuntary separations, access is removed during or immediately after the conversation. For role eliminations with a transition period, the access plan may be different, but it must be explicit.

If the employee is remote, confirm People Operations has the right mailing address and personal email before the meeting. Do not ask the employee to return equipment verbally without the written instructions. Equipment return belongs in the follow-up packet and the device process.

## Afterward

Document what was communicated. Inform only those who need to know. For the remaining team, share a respectful transition message focused on work coverage. Do not share private performance or personal details. See [underperformance and terminations](underperformance-and-terminations.md) for the required steps before a performance-based termination.

After a difficult conversation, managers often want to decompress with peers. Be careful. You may ask your manager or People Operations for support, but do not turn a sensitive conversation into Slack commentary. The employee's dignity matters even when the conversation was hard.

Follow through quickly. If you promised a written recap, send it the same day. If you promised to ask IT about access, open the ticket. If you promised a growth plan, draft it. The trust cost of a hard conversation gets worse when the manager disappears afterward.

## Manager Self-Check

Before any difficult conversation, ask yourself:

- Can I state the issue in one or two plain sentences?
- Do I have concrete examples?
- Have I separated facts from interpretation?
- Am I the right person to deliver this message?
- Does People Operations, IT, Finance, or Legal need to be involved?
- What decision is already made, and what is still open?
- What should the employee do or expect next?

If you cannot answer these questions, keep preparing. A rushed hard conversation usually creates a second hard conversation about the first one.
