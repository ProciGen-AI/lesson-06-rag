# 1:1s

## Purpose

A 1:1 is the manager's most important recurring operating rhythm. It is not a status meeting with a different name. Status belongs in Jira, Salesforce, Slack, or the team board. A 1:1 is where the report can raise concerns, get context, discuss priorities, receive coaching, and build enough trust that hard topics do not appear only during reviews.

Managers own the existence of the meeting. Reports should help shape the agenda. If you manage someone, do not make them chase you for basic manager time.

At NovaOps, 1:1s are also one of the main ways remote-first management stays humane. The company has people in Israel, Germany, the United Kingdom, and the United States, so managers cannot rely on overhearing stress in an office or catching someone after a meeting. A steady 1:1 rhythm gives employees a place to raise weak signals before they become project failures, customer escalations, or performance surprises.

## Cadence

For a brand-new report, hold a 30-60 minute 1:1 every week for the first few months. New employees, employees new to a role, and L1-L2 reports usually need more time because they are still building context and judgment. Once someone is well onboarded, performing, and has stable work, the cadence can drop to about once a month if both people agree.

A senior employee in a high-ambiguity project may still need weekly time. A junior employee in a calm period may need a shorter weekly check-in. Use judgment, but do not let convenience quietly become neglect.

The first three months are the highest-touch period. A new Customer Success Manager such as Maya Cohen should have weekly 1:1s while learning Salesforce, Webex, SupportDesk, customer health rituals, and the escalation path. A new engineer should have weekly 1:1s while learning GitHub review norms, Jira planning, local development setup, and production boundaries. A contractor may need a shorter cadence, but still needs a named manager check-in while access, scope, and end dates are active.

Monthly 1:1s are appropriate only when the report is well onboarded, performing, and not carrying a new or risky scope. Do not move to monthly because the manager is busy. Move to monthly because the employee has enough context, other feedback channels are working, and both people agree the lighter cadence is still useful. If work becomes ambiguous, performance changes, the employee returns from leave, or the team is reorganized, move back to weekly or every other week for a while.

Skipping a 1:1 is different from rescheduling it. If a customer incident, illness, holiday, or travel conflict causes a cancellation, reschedule within the same week when practical. If you miss two in a row, send a note acknowledging it and offer a new time. Silence from the manager can read as avoidance.

## Agenda

Ask the report to add topics before the meeting. Useful prompts include: what is blocked, what decision do you need, what feedback would help, what is getting harder, what do you want more or less of, and what should I know that I may not see.

Managers should bring context from leadership, feedback from recent work, and any priority changes. End with clear actions: who will do what by when. If the meeting produces no actions for several weeks, check whether the cadence or format needs to change.

Good agenda items are specific. "Talk about Jira" is vague. "I need a decision on whether the delayed billing API work should move after the Salesforce import bug" is useful. "Feedback" is vague. "How did I handle the Webex customer escalation with Rachel Stein?" is useful. Managers should coach employees to bring the decision, context, and deadline.

A useful 1:1 usually has three layers: current work, development, and working relationship. Current work covers priorities, blockers, risks, and decisions. Development covers feedback, skills, title expectations, promotion readiness, or stretch assignments. Working relationship covers communication, trust, workload, and what the manager can do differently. Not every meeting needs all three, but a quarter of only status updates is a warning sign.

Managers should bring feedback from real work systems. For Engineering, that may be a GitHub review pattern, a Jira delivery issue, or an incident note. For Sales and Customer Success, it may be Salesforce hygiene, renewal quality, customer communication, or escalation judgment. For HR, IT, and Finance, it may be follow-through on onboarding tasks, access approvals, procurement decisions, or sensitive record handling.

## Questions Worth Asking

Rotate questions so the conversation does not become stale. Useful prompts include:

- What changed since we last spoke that I might not see?
- Which decision is waiting on me?
- Which project has the most hidden risk?
- Where are you spending energy that is not visible in Jira, Salesforce, GitHub, or Slack?
- What feedback have you received from peers or customers?
- What should we stop doing?
- What do you want to learn or own next?
- Is there any access, equipment, or process issue slowing you down?
- Are you getting enough context from me?

For a new report, ask more basic questions: which names are still confusing, which system is hardest to use, which meeting feels unclear, and which first-month expectation needs rewriting. For an experienced report, ask more judgment questions: what tradeoff would you make, where are we over-investing, and who else needs context.

## Remote Practice

Use Webex for conversations that need nuance. Keep shared notes only if they help the employee; do not create a secret file of surprises. Sensitive performance notes belong in the appropriate People Operations process, not in a casual private document.

Canceling occasionally is fine. Repeated cancellations tell the report they are optional. Reschedule within the same week whenever possible.

Video is not mandatory for every 1:1, but managers should notice when audio-only or chat-only becomes avoidance. Use Webex when discussing performance, conflict, growth, leave return, role changes, or personal workload stress. Slack is fine for quick rescheduling or a lightweight check-in, but it is not enough for difficult feedback.

Time zones matter. A manager in the United Kingdom should not always place the burden on a report in Israel or the United States. Rotate inconvenient times if needed. If a report works a flexible schedule because of caregiving, leave return, or local holidays, set a stable time that respects that reality.

## Notes and Follow-Up

If you keep notes, tell the employee what kind of notes they are. Shared action notes are encouraged. Private manager notes should be factual and minimal. Do not keep sensitive medical, family, or protected-leave details in a personal note. If a topic might become formal performance management, move the relevant facts into the People Operations process.

At the end of the meeting, name the follow-up. "I'll ask Amir about GitHub access by Friday" is better than "I'll look into it." "You will draft the customer renewal plan before Tuesday and I will review it by Wednesday" is better than "keep me posted." If the action belongs in Jira, Salesforce, BambooHR, or the IT request portal, put it there instead of letting the 1:1 note become a hidden task system.

## Warning Signs

A 1:1 practice is weak when the manager does all the talking, the agenda is always status, hard feedback appears only in reviews, the report never brings problems, or every meeting ends with the same unresolved blocker. It is also weak when the manager uses the meeting to vent about leadership, leak confidential information, or ask the report to manage the manager's emotions.

If the employee stops engaging, do not assume they have nothing to say. Ask whether the format is useful, whether there is a trust issue, or whether another channel would help. If the employee raises serious concerns about harassment, discrimination, security, fraud, self-harm, or protected leave, stop trying to solve it alone and involve People Operations or the appropriate owner.
