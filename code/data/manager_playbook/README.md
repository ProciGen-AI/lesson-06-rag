# NovaOps Manager Playbook

## What This Is

This playbook is for NovaOps managers. It explains how we expect managers to run 1:1s, give feedback, hire, onboard, recognize work, handle performance, support promotion, and navigate difficult conversations. It is intentionally practical. The goal is not to make every manager sound the same; it is to give employees a consistent management floor no matter which department they join.

The employee handbook contains company-wide policies. This playbook contains manager-only guidance. Managers may use it to answer questions about how to act, but should still rely on People Operations for employment law, compensation, leave, investigations, and separation paperwork.

NovaOps is a hybrid and remote-first B2B SaaS company with employees and contractors working across Israel, Germany, the United Kingdom, the United States, and other approved locations. Because work is distributed, management practice cannot depend on hallway context, informal access to leaders, or one office's habits. This playbook turns expected manager behavior into written operating guidance so a Sales manager in the United States, a Customer Success manager in Israel, an Engineering leader in the United Kingdom, and an IT manager in Germany can make comparable decisions.

Use this document as the entrance to the manager corpus. It explains what belongs in the manager playbook, who may rely on it, how it differs from employee-facing policy, and where managers should route questions that require HR, IT, Finance, Legal, or executive review. The detailed documents linked below contain the procedural guidance. This README gives the map, the boundaries, and the retrieval-friendly facts managers and support agents are most likely to need.

## Manager Promise

Management at NovaOps is a service role. You create clarity, make tradeoffs visible, protect focus, develop people, and address problems early. You do not need to be the loudest expert in the room. You do need to be honest, prepared, and consistent.

Good managers also know when a question belongs elsewhere. A leave question belongs with People Operations. A privileged access question belongs with IT. A new recurring spend question belongs with Finance. Routing work to the right owner is part of managing well, not a sign that you lack authority.

If a document in this playbook conflicts with a local law, employment agreement, or written direction from People Operations, follow the stricter or more specific requirement and ask People Operations to update the playbook.

## Manager Charter

The NovaOps manager charter is the minimum standard for anyone with direct reports, dotted-line reports, contractors, interns, or a formal hiring-manager role.

Managers create clarity. Each report should know the outcomes that matter, the decision rights they hold, the systems they should use, the evidence that will define success, and the tradeoffs the manager has already made. Clarity is especially important in remote-first work because people cannot infer priorities from who is physically nearby.

Managers protect fair process. Similar situations should receive similar treatment even when the employee is senior, persuasive, urgent, visible, local to the manager, or close to a customer. Fair process applies to hiring, role changes, promotions, compensation input, access requests, expenses, recognition, leave coverage, performance plans, and terminations.

Managers develop people. Development includes regular 1:1s, specific feedback, coaching, stretch opportunities, thoughtful delegation, promotion readiness conversations, and honest discussion of gaps. Development is not the same as protecting employees from hard feedback. Employees grow faster when the manager is clear, kind, and direct.

Managers protect trust and confidentiality. Employee information is shared only with people who need it for a legitimate business, legal, HR, security, or finance purpose. Medical details, leave details, compensation details, performance concerns, and investigation facts should not live in casual Slack messages, private side documents, or hallway-style remote chatter.

Managers route specialized decisions. A manager can confirm business need, context, and priority. That does not mean the manager can approve every downstream consequence. HR determines employment process, IT determines access safety, Finance determines spend approval and compensation-cost handling, and Legal or executive leadership may be required for high-risk matters.

Managers model remote-first behavior. Use written context, inclusive meeting times, explicit decision records, and healthy work boundaries. Do not reward constant availability as though it were the same as impact. Do not create an inner circle made of people who share the manager's time zone.

Managers address problems early. Serious gaps should not wait for a performance review. Access blockers, unclear priorities, repeated missed commitments, customer escalation patterns, security concerns, employee conflict, and workload overload should be surfaced while they are still fixable.

## Source And Corpus Boundaries

The manager playbook is one corpus inside the NovaOps knowledge system. It is distinct from employee-facing handbook material, vendor contracts, support tickets, HR records, Finance data, IT system records, customer notes, and individual personnel files.

Use the manager playbook for questions such as:

- How often should a brand-new report have 1:1s?
- What should a manager do before starting a correction plan?
- Who should be involved before a termination conversation?
- What belongs in a promotion packet?
- How should a manager give feedback after a missed customer commitment?
- How should managers handle sensitive employee information?
- What should a manager do when an access request, expense request, or leave question falls outside the manager's authority?

Do not use the manager playbook as the source of truth for:

- Employee benefit amounts, payroll deductions, tax treatment, leave eligibility, severance calculations, or legal notices.
- Current system access state, SaaS seat counts, procurement records, vendor renewal dates, or live incident status.
- Individual employee records, medical records, investigation records, compensation records, or private HR case notes.
- Final authority for country-specific employment law, immigration, payroll, or regulated security decisions.

When a question crosses corpuses, answer only the manager-practice part from this playbook and route the policy, record, or legal part to the right owner. For example, a manager can explain that People Operations must prepare separation paperwork and that the manager schedules and delivers the final termination conversation. The manager should not calculate final pay or invent severance terms from memory.

If a retrieval system combines corpuses, preserve the audience boundary. A user asking as an employee should not receive manager-only performance-process instructions that they are not authorized to view. A manager asking for employee-policy amounts should be routed to the employee handbook or People Operations rather than this playbook.

Do not forward manager-only documents into open employee channels, customer emails, vendor emails, public issue trackers, or external workspaces. When an employee needs to understand a decision, explain the decision in employee-appropriate language rather than forwarding the internal manager process.

## Remote-First Manager Expectations

Remote-first management at NovaOps means managers write down context, decisions, and expectations so people can work across time zones. It does not mean every conversation should happen asynchronously. Use Webex or in-person conversation where available for nuance, performance feedback, conflict, leave return, role changes, compensation disappointment, or any conversation that could affect trust.

Respect time zones across Israel, Germany, the United Kingdom, the United States, and other approved locations. Rotate inconvenient meeting times when a team spans regions. Do not always place the burden on the person farthest from headquarters or the manager's own calendar. Use scheduled messages when writing late, and make clear whether a message is urgent or simply captured while the thought is fresh.

Keep status in the operating system where the work lives. Jira is for planned Product and Engineering work. GitHub is for code review and technical change. Salesforce and SupportDesk are for customer-facing work. BambooHR is for HR records. Slack is useful for coordination, but it should not be the only place a formal decision lives.

Create inclusive access to information. Important context, feedback, recognition, stretch opportunities, and promotion evidence should not depend on who happens to be online at the same hour as the manager. Share decision records, meeting notes, and action items in a place the whole relevant team can reach.

Model sustainable work. Do not praise weekend work as the default. Do not imply that being online late is a sign of loyalty. Production incidents, customer escalations, payroll blockers, security concerns, and urgent access removals may require fast response. Normal planning work, feedback, and status updates should respect local working days whenever practical.

Notice weak signals. In a distributed company, a manager may not visually notice stress, confusion, isolation, or conflict. Regular 1:1s, written check-ins, and clear escalation paths make it easier for employees to surface risk before it becomes a formal problem.

## Common Failure Modes

The most common manager failure mode is waiting too long. Waiting turns coaching gaps into performance cases, access blockers into missed commitments, customer confusion into renewal risk, and workload problems into burnout. Managers should address small signals before the formal process is the only option left.

Another failure mode is making private exceptions. A manager may want to help a strong employee, a favorite candidate, a senior engineer, or a high-pressure salesperson by bending the process. Private exceptions create fairness, compliance, security, and budget risk. If an exception may be justified, route it to the owner and document the decision.

A third failure mode is confusing manager authority with specialist authority. Managers can approve the business need for a tool, but IT decides whether access is safe. Managers can identify a role need, but Finance and People Operations approve headcount, compensation, and offer process. Managers can give feedback, but People Operations must be involved before formal warnings, correction plans, and terminations.

A fourth failure mode is using vague language. Phrases such as "not strategic," "bad attitude," "poor fit," "needs polish," or "not senior enough" are not useful unless the manager can connect them to observable behavior and business impact. Use examples from work: missed Salesforce updates, incomplete Jira acceptance criteria, repeated unreviewed GitHub changes, poor customer follow-up, unclear project ownership, or failure to follow access process.

A fifth failure mode is overusing Slack for sensitive topics. Slack is useful for quick coordination. It is not the right place for termination details, medical information, compensation debate, serious performance warnings, investigation facts, or confidential employee complaints. Use the appropriate system and route to People Operations, IT, or Finance.

A sixth failure mode is creating dependency through over-coaching. A manager who reviews every small decision may feel supportive while preventing the employee from building judgment. Good coaching keeps the employee accountable for the work and gives them a clear next practice step.

A seventh failure mode is hiding operational work. Remote teams often notice launches and incidents but miss quiet prevention, onboarding work, documentation, access cleanup, QA, support triage, Finance controls, and HR process quality. Managers should recognize visible and invisible work when it creates real value.

An eighth failure mode is treating the review cycle as the feedback system. Formal reviews in February and August should summarize known evidence. They should not be the first time an employee hears serious praise, disappointment, promotion gaps, or performance risk.

## Escalation Paths

For serious performance risk, contact People Operations as soon as the serious lag is identified and before telling the employee that a formal process has started. Work with People Operations to determine whether the situation is coaching, unclear expectations, missing access, leave or accommodation related, conflict, misconduct, or formal performance management.

For a correction plan, work with People Operations before delivery. A correction plan should include the gap, examples, expected standard, measurable benchmarks, support offered, timeline, check-in cadence, and consequences. People Operations checks in weekly on progress during a formal correction plan.

For termination due to underperformance, the manager schedules and delivers the final termination conversation after the approved process and evidence review. People Operations prepares the materials and either joins or remains immediately available. After the conversation, People Operations follows up on last-day logistics, paperwork, benefits, severance if applicable, and equipment return. IT handles access removal through Okta and related systems.

For harassment, discrimination, retaliation, safety concerns, self-harm, fraud, threats, serious misconduct, or protected-leave concerns, escalate immediately to People Operations. Do not promise absolute secrecy. Tell the employee that you will share only with people who need to know so the company can respond appropriately.

For security incidents, confidential-data exposure, suspicious access, privileged-access misuse, lost devices, or accidental access to BambooHR, AWS, GitHub, Salesforce, Slack, SupportDesk, or other sensitive systems, escalate immediately to IT. If the incident also involves employee conduct or HR records, involve People Operations as well.

For urgent customer or production impact, use the team's incident or customer-escalation path and then make sure the people-management follow-up happens afterward. Customer urgency does not erase the need for fair feedback, accurate records, and sustainable coverage.

For spend, procurement, paid-tool expansion, recognition above manager limit, equipment exceptions, or recurring costs, escalate to Finance before committing. If IT also owns the tool or security review, Finance approval and IT approval may both be required.

For promotion, role changes, or compensation implications, involve People Operations and Finance before promising an outcome. Promotion proposals run in March and September, take effect April 1 and October 1 when approved, and are not final until calibration and Finance review are complete.

For uncertainty about which path applies, start with People Operations for employee-impacting issues, IT for access or security issues, Finance for spend issues, and the department leader or COO for business-priority conflicts. If more than one owner is needed, bring them into the same thread or workflow so the employee is not asked to coordinate the escalation personally.

## Manager Decision Checklist

Before acting, ask:

- Is this a manager-practice question, an employee-policy question, a live record question, or a specialist approval question?
- Is the employee allowed to receive the information I am about to share?
- Does the decision affect employment, compensation, leave, access, spend, security, customer commitments, or legal risk?
- Have I documented the facts in the right system?
- Is the evidence specific enough that another manager could understand the decision?
- Have I separated my authority to confirm business need from another team's authority to approve risk, cost, or process?
- Does this need People Operations, IT, Finance, Legal, a department leader, or the COO before I speak?
- If I am making an exception, who approved it and where is the approval recorded?

If the answer is unclear, slow down and route. A short delay for the right owner is better than a fast promise the company cannot honor.
