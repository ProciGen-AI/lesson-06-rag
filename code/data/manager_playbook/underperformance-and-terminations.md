# Underperformance and Terminations

## Act Early

When a report is seriously lagging, contact People Operations as soon as you identify the issue and before you talk to the report as though a formal process has started. Early HR involvement protects the employee, the manager, and the company. It also helps distinguish a skill gap from unclear expectations, leave, accommodation needs, team conflict, or a role mismatch.

Do not wait until you are already convinced termination is inevitable. The earlier the process starts, the more likely the employee has a fair chance to improve.

Early does not mean dramatic. A manager can contact People Operations to ask, "Is this coaching, unclear expectations, or the beginning of formal performance management?" without telling the employee their job is at risk that day. Sara Ben-David or Sara Cohen may ask for examples from Jira, GitHub, Salesforce, customer notes, 1:1 notes, or project plans. Bring evidence, not a mood.

People Operations should be involved before a manager uses phrases like formal warning, correction plan, performance plan, final warning, or job in jeopardy. Those phrases have process meaning. If the employee may need leave, accommodation, role clarification, or a manager change, People Operations helps separate those questions from performance evidence.

## Before Formal Action

Before moving to a correction plan, check the basics. Was the expectation written down? Did the employee have the required system access? Were priorities stable enough to judge the work? Did the manager give timely feedback? Was the employee newly onboarded, returning from leave, or moved into a different role? Is the gap individual performance, or is the team asking one person to compensate for a broken process?

This review should be honest. If a Customer Success Specialist is missing customer follow-up because Salesforce access was delayed, that is not the same as ignoring customer commitments. If an engineer is late because the Jira ticket hides a cross-team dependency, that is not the same as poor execution. If a Sales Operations employee repeatedly exports data outside the approved workflow after coaching, that may be both a performance and policy issue.

Document what you know and what you do not know. People Operations may tell you to coach more specifically, clarify the role, adjust workload, investigate a conduct concern, or proceed to a formal correction plan.

## Correction Plan

Work with People Operations to design a correction plan. The plan should include the performance gap, examples, expected standard, measurable benchmarks, support offered, timeline, check-in cadence, and consequences if the gap is not corrected. Most plans run 30 to 60 days, but the timeline should match the role and severity.

Deliver the plan with direct feedback. Make clear if the employee's job is in jeopardy. Avoid coded language such as fit concerns unless you can explain the actual behavior or outcome. After delivery, meet regularly with the employee and document progress. People Operations checks in weekly on progress during a formal correction plan.

The plan should be specific enough that another manager could understand whether it was met. "Communicate better" is not a benchmark. "Post a Salesforce renewal risk summary by Thursday noon for every account in the red segment" is a benchmark. "Improve technical judgment" is not a benchmark. "For the next two backend changes, write the migration plan, test plan, rollback plan, and risk summary before opening the GitHub pull request" is a benchmark.

Support should also be concrete. Support may include pairing with a senior teammate, reducing project load, clarifying decision rights, weekly review of customer notes, a written checklist, or a temporary change in meeting cadence. Support does not mean the manager does the employee's work. If the manager has to rewrite every deliverable, the plan should say that independent execution is part of the expected improvement.

Most correction plans include weekly manager check-ins and weekly People Operations check-ins with the manager. In some cases People Operations may also check in with the employee. Managers should send short written updates after each checkpoint: what benchmark was reviewed, what evidence exists, what improved, what remains below standard, and whether the timeline still makes sense.

## Evidence Quality

Use recent and relevant evidence. Strong evidence includes missed customer commitments, repeated production-quality issues, incomplete Jira work against agreed acceptance criteria, GitHub review patterns, failure to follow approved security or access procedures, poor Salesforce hygiene after coaching, or repeated failure to own agreed outcomes. Weak evidence includes personality labels, gossip, surprise complaints, or frustration that the employee works differently than the manager.

Do not pad the record. A correction plan should focus on the performance gap that matters. Adding every small annoyance makes the process feel unfair and distracts from the real issue. If there are three serious problems, group them clearly. If there is one serious problem, name that one.

## During the Plan

During a correction plan, keep feedback direct and current. If the employee meets a benchmark, say so. If they miss a benchmark, say so quickly and explain the consequence for the plan. Do not wait until the end to reveal that you believed the plan was failing.

If the employee raises new information such as a medical issue, protected leave need, harassment concern, lack of required access, or a conflict with another employee, pause and involve People Operations. Pausing to review new information does not mean the concern disappears. It means the company handles the issue through the right process.

If performance improves enough to close the plan, tell the employee in writing what changed, what standard must continue, and when normal management cadence resumes. Keep the documentation in BambooHR or the People Operations workflow. Do not keep private shadow files.

## When Termination May Be Appropriate

Termination may be appropriate when the employee does not meet the correction plan, when the gap is too severe for a normal plan, when the role is eliminated, or when misconduct occurs. Serious misconduct, harassment, security violations, fraud, or threats require immediate People Operations involvement and may not follow the normal correction timeline.

Managers should not calculate final pay, severance, benefits, or legal terms themselves. People Operations and Finance confirm those details.

Termination for underperformance should be based on the approved plan and evidence. The final decision should answer four questions: what expectation was not met, what support was provided, what evidence shows the gap remains, and why continued employment is not appropriate. If the manager cannot answer those questions, more review is needed before a final conversation.

Role elimination is different from underperformance. A role-elimination message should not include performance criticism. A performance termination should not be disguised as a restructure. If both business change and performance concerns exist, People Operations will decide how to communicate accurately and fairly.

Misconduct may require immediate action. If the issue involves security, confidential data, harassment, fraud, threats, or serious customer harm, contact People Operations and, when relevant, IT immediately. Do not conduct your own side investigation in Slack or ask peers to collect stories.

## Final Conversation

The manager schedules and delivers the final termination conversation. People Operations prepares the materials and either joins or remains immediately available. Keep the conversation brief, clear, and respectful. State that employment is ending, the effective date, and the approved high-level reason. Do not debate the decision or negotiate terms in the meeting.

After the conversation, People Operations follows up on last-day logistics, paperwork, benefits, severance if applicable, and equipment return. IT handles access removal through Okta and related systems. The manager owns transition planning and a respectful team communication.

The final conversation should usually be on Webex unless local law or a local office situation calls for a different format. Do not schedule it at the end of a long team meeting, during a customer escalation, or as a mysterious calendar invite with many attendees. The meeting should include the manager and People Operations unless People Operations explicitly directs otherwise.

Use the approved script. It should be plain: "We have decided to end your employment with NovaOps. Your last working day is [date]. The reason is [approved high-level reason]. People Operations will walk you through written details after this call." Then pause. The employee may be shocked, angry, quiet, or practical. Listen respectfully, but do not debate the decision, recite the entire correction plan, or make new promises.

Do not mention severance amounts unless People Operations has told you to read a specific line. Do not promise references, future contracting, access extensions, immigration outcomes, equipment exceptions, or continued benefits. If the employee asks questions you cannot answer, say People Operations will follow up in writing.

## Team Communication and Transition

After the employee has been informed, tell only the people who need to know. A team note should be respectful and minimal: the employee is leaving NovaOps, transition ownership is assigned, and customer or project coverage is clear. Do not share private performance details. Do not say the person chose to leave if they did not.

For customer-facing roles, decide who updates Salesforce, who owns customer follow-up, and whether any Webex meetings need to be reassigned. For Engineering, decide who owns open GitHub pull requests, Jira tickets, operational alerts, and code areas. For HR, IT, and Finance, identify recurring tasks that might otherwise disappear quietly.

After the transition plan is stable, review what the manager and team can learn. Sometimes the answer is better coaching. Sometimes it is clearer hiring criteria, better onboarding, sharper access provisioning, more realistic workload, or earlier feedback. The review is not about relitigating the employee's departure; it is about making the system less likely to repeat the same failure.
