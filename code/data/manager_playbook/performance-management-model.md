# Performance Management Model

## Principles

Performance management at NovaOps is continuous, evidence-based, and direct. The goal is to help people do strong work and to address gaps early when they appear. Formal reviews matter, but they should summarize an ongoing conversation rather than reveal surprises.

Managers are responsible for setting expectations. Employees are responsible for engaging with those expectations, surfacing blockers, and doing the work. People Operations is responsible for process quality, documentation, and fairness.

The model is meant to keep performance conversations from becoming mysterious. A report should know whether they are doing the job, where they are growing, and what evidence the manager is using. Managers should be able to explain performance in plain work terms: customer outcomes, product quality, delivery, collaboration, security, judgment, reliability, and role scope.

Remote-first work makes explicit performance management more important. Managers do not see every effort or every struggle. Employees do not overhear every priority change. The model works only when expectations are written, feedback is timely, and evidence comes from actual work rather than visibility, personality, or calendar presence.

## Performance States

We use four working states. On Track means the employee is meeting the expectations of the role and level. Growing means the employee is making progress but still needs support in specific areas, often because they are new to the role or taking on new scope. Exceptional Impact means the employee is delivering sustained results above the current level or creating unusual leverage. At Risk means there is a serious gap that could affect continued employment if not corrected.

These states are not permanent labels. They are a way to decide what support or action is needed now.

On Track does not mean perfect. It means the employee is meeting the meaningful expectations of the role with normal feedback and normal support. An On Track engineer may still receive code review comments. An On Track Customer Success Manager may still need help with a difficult renewal. An On Track HR or IT employee may still need escalation on an unusual case.

Growing is common for new hires, people taking on a new scope, and employees stretching into the next level. Growing should have a clear support plan. If the manager cannot explain what the employee is growing toward, the state is too vague. Growing should not be used as a polite label for At Risk.

Exceptional Impact means sustained leverage, not one dramatic week. It may show up as a designer simplifying a complex customer workflow across product areas, an engineer reducing production risk across services, an IT manager improving access review quality, or a Customer Success leader turning recurring escalations into product feedback that changes roadmap priority.

At Risk means the gap is serious enough that continued employment, current role fit, or current level may be in question if the gap is not corrected. At Risk requires People Operations involvement before the manager presents the issue as a formal warning. The manager may continue coaching while People Operations helps decide the process.

## Setting Expectations

Every report should have three layers of expectations: role outcomes, level expectations, and current priorities. Role outcomes explain why the job exists. Level expectations explain the judgment and scope expected for the title. Current priorities explain what matters now. A manager who only gives task lists is not fully setting expectations.

Good expectations are observable. "Own the Webex license cleanup workflow and give IT a weekly risk summary" is observable. "Be more proactive" is not. "For the next two GitHub changes, include test evidence and rollback notes before review" is observable. "Improve engineering maturity" is not. "Keep Salesforce renewal notes current by Friday close for red accounts" is observable. "Care more about customers" is not.

Managers should revisit expectations when work changes. A role change, transfer, new manager, return from leave, major incident, or new customer escalation can make old expectations incomplete. Do not judge someone against an expectation you never updated.

## Manager Actions

For On Track employees, keep priorities clear and continue feedback. For Growing employees, provide coaching, examples, and follow-up dates. For Exceptional Impact employees, consider expanded scope, recognition, or a promotion discussion. For At Risk employees, contact People Operations as soon as you identify a serious lag, before you frame the issue to the report as a formal warning.

A correction plan should include the gap, expected standard, measurable benchmarks, support offered, timeline, and consequences if the gap is not corrected. People Operations should review the plan before delivery.

For On Track employees, the manager's job is not to disappear. Keep giving useful feedback, make sure the employee has appropriate scope, and check whether the current work is still aligned with business priorities. On Track employees can become disengaged if they receive only silence.

For Growing employees, write the growth target. A Product Manager moving into a more technical area might need coaching on Jira acceptance criteria, dependency mapping, and customer impact tradeoffs. A Support Specialist moving toward senior scope might need coaching on escalation judgment, difficult customer communication, and product feedback quality.

For Exceptional Impact employees, do not assume praise is enough. Consider whether the work should be recognized, whether scope should expand, whether the employee needs protection from overload, or whether the evidence belongs in a promotion discussion. Exceptional Impact can become burnout if the manager rewards it only with more urgent work.

For At Risk employees, act promptly and calmly. Do not threaten termination casually. Do not imply a formal process before People Operations is involved. Do collect evidence, clarify expectations, and continue giving direct feedback.

## Evidence

Use evidence from work: outcomes, customer impact, code review, Jira delivery, Salesforce hygiene, collaboration, reliability, quality, and communication. Avoid vague labels such as not strategic or bad attitude unless you can explain the behavior and impact.

See [performance reviews](performance-reviews.md) for formal review cycles and [underperformance and terminations](underperformance-and-terminations.md) for serious gaps.

Evidence should be recent enough to matter and broad enough to be fair. One mistake in a new tool is usually coaching. A repeated pattern after feedback may be performance evidence. One heroic save does not erase months of missed commitments. A quiet employee may be performing strongly even if they are not loud in Slack.

Examples of strong evidence:

- Jira work was completed against agreed acceptance criteria, with clear updates when scope changed.
- GitHub pull requests included tests, review responses, and risk notes appropriate to the change.
- Salesforce renewal notes were current, accurate, and useful to Sales and Customer Success.
- A manager or senior employee could trust the person to handle a customer escalation without creating confusion.
- The employee identified a risk early and proposed a workable mitigation.
- A process improvement reduced repeated IT, HR, Finance, or customer-support work.

Examples of weak evidence:

- "They do not seem excited."
- "They are not like the previous person in the role."
- "They ask too many questions" without explaining whether the questions are repeated, late, or blocking.
- "They are not visible" when the work outputs are strong.
- "People are frustrated" without names, examples, or behaviors People Operations can review.

## Access, Tools, and Process Blockers

Before rating performance, check whether the employee had the tools and decisions needed to do the work. Missing Salesforce access, a delayed Webex license, stale manager data, unclear GitHub permissions, or an unresolved AWS approval can make a capable employee look slow. Managers should resolve those blockers or account for them in the evaluation.

Process blockers do not excuse every performance issue. An employee can still be responsible for communicating risk, asking for help, and doing available work. But managers should not treat systemic friction as individual failure. If three employees are missing the same benchmark, investigate the system before escalating all three people.

## Calibration

Managers should discuss performance patterns with their manager and People Operations before formal reviews or major decisions. Calibration checks whether similar evidence is being treated similarly across teams. It also catches bias toward employees who are nearby, loud, senior, or personally similar to the manager.

Calibration does not mean flattening everyone to the same rating. It means the evidence and expectations are comparable. A Staff Engineer and a Support Specialist have different ladders, but both deserve a clear explanation of scope, impact, and gaps.

## Documentation

Keep documentation factual, specific, and in the appropriate system. BambooHR and People Operations workflows are for formal reviews, correction plans, and sensitive employment records. Jira, GitHub, Salesforce, and project documents are work systems, not substitute HR files. Private notes may help a manager remember facts, but they should not become a secret performance record full of impressions.

The standard is simple: if the employee read the performance evidence later, it should be recognizable, specific, and connected to work. They may disagree with it, but it should not feel like a hidden story.
