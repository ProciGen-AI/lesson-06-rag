# Manager Standards

## The Baseline

Every NovaOps manager is expected to provide clarity, consistency, and care. These standards apply whether you manage Sales, Customer Success, Product, Engineering, HR, IT, Finance, or a cross-functional team. Your style can be your own. The employee experience should still meet a common floor.

The common floor matters because NovaOps is remote-first and distributed across Israel, Germany, the United Kingdom, and the United States. Employees should not need to be in the same office, time zone, or social circle as their manager to understand what matters. A manager's job is to make work legible: outcomes, priorities, decision rights, feedback, growth, and risk.

Management is not a seniority badge. It is an operating responsibility. You are accountable for the conditions in which your team does work: the quality of goals, the speed of decisions, the honesty of feedback, the fairness of process, and the health of communication. You do not have to be the best individual contributor in every conversation. You do have to create the context that lets strong contributors do good work.

## What Managers Must Do

Set outcomes. Each report should know what matters most, how success will be evaluated, and which decisions they can make independently. If priorities conflict, help choose. Do not let employees guess which urgent request wins.

Hold regular 1:1s. New reports need weekly 30-60 minute 1:1s for the first few months. Stable, fully ramped reports may move to about monthly if that works for both of you. See [1:1s](1-1s.md).

Give feedback close to the work. Praise should name impact. Corrective feedback should name the gap and the next step. Serious concerns should not wait for a review cycle.

Protect fair process. Use the same promotion, review, hiring, access, and expense rules for everyone. Do not create private exceptions because someone is senior, persuasive, or nearby.

Document formal decisions. Performance reviews, correction plans, promotions, leave accommodations, and termination decisions belong in the approved People Operations process.

Run the operating rhythm. Each manager should maintain a team cadence that fits the work: planning, 1:1s, async updates, feedback, review preparation, and risk escalation. Engineering managers should know where Jira work and GitHub review are stuck. Sales and Customer Success managers should know which Salesforce accounts are at risk and which customer commitments need help. HR, IT, and Finance managers should know which operational workflows are waiting on approvals, documents, access, or budget decisions.

Make approval paths clear. Manager approval confirms business need; it does not override IT, Finance, People Operations, or security review. If a report asks for GitHub organization admin, AWS admin, Salesforce export, BambooHR HR access, a new paid tool, or equipment above the threshold, the manager should route the request correctly rather than saying yes informally.

Protect focus. Remote work makes it easy to create a day of fragments: Slack pings, Webex calls, Jira comments, Salesforce emergencies, and one-off approvals. Managers should decide what actually matters and reduce noise where they can. If everything is urgent, the manager has not done the prioritization work.

Develop people. Every report should know what growth looks like for their role and level. A manager should be able to explain the difference between current-level strength, next-level evidence, and promotion readiness. Growth is not only promotion; it can include broader judgment, cleaner execution, better customer handling, stronger technical depth, or more reliable operations.

Escalate early. Managers should involve People Operations for leave, accommodations, harassment or discrimination concerns, compensation, promotion process, serious performance gaps, and terminations. They should involve IT for access, device, security, SaaS, and privileged-system issues. They should involve Finance for spend thresholds, recurring cost, procurement, and budget questions. Escalation is part of good management.

## What Managers Must Not Do

Do not approve system access just because it is convenient. Manager approval confirms business need; IT still reviews risk. Do not approve spend outside Finance thresholds. Do not ask employees for medical details. Do not promise compensation, promotion, severance, immigration outcomes, or termination terms without People Operations or Finance approval.

Do not use private influence to bypass process. A manager should not ask Amir Haddad to add access without a ticket because it is faster, ask Tom Baker to approve spend after the purchase, or ask People Operations to backdate a decision because the conversation already happened. The process exists to protect employees, customers, and the company.

Do not hide behind process either. "People Ops says no" is not a substitute for a manager explaining the evidence or business reason. "Finance blocked it" is not enough if the manager never clarified the budget case. A good manager owns the message even when another function owns the approval.

Do not make work personal. You may care about reports as people, but you are still in a role with authority. Avoid favoritism, social pressure, gossip, and private arrangements that others would reasonably view as unequal. Important information should not be available only to employees who chat with you after hours.

Do not let ambiguity become performance evidence. If expectations were unclear, access was missing, or priorities changed without explanation, fix that before judging the employee harshly. Strong managers distinguish individual gaps from system gaps.

## Remote-First Standards

Remote-first management requires written context. Important decisions should not live only in Webex memory. Use Jira for planned delivery, GitHub for engineering review, Salesforce for account and customer work, BambooHR for HR workflows, and the NovaOps wiki for decision records that need to outlast Slack. Slack is useful, but it is not a durable operating system.

Managers should respect local working hours. The core collaboration window helps, but it does not erase time zones. If a meeting is repeatedly painful for one location, rotate it or find an async alternative. Do not praise employees for always being online late; that teaches the team the wrong lesson.

Remote-first also means noticing isolation. A new hire who is quiet in Slack may be focused, confused, or stuck. A high performer who stops asking questions may be overloaded. A returning employee may need context rebuilt. Managers should not surveil; they should create enough contact that weak signals have somewhere to go.

## Manager Checklists

Weekly, a manager should know: what matters most, which work is blocked, which decision waits on them, which employee needs feedback, which customer or internal risk needs escalation, and whether any team member is overloaded.

Monthly, a manager should review: 1:1 cadence, hiring or onboarding obligations, access and spend requests, recognition, growth plans, and whether any operational process is relying on one person's memory.

Each review cycle, a manager should prepare evidence early, calibrate fairly, explain decisions clearly, and record final reviews in BambooHR. Each promotion cycle, the manager should separate readiness from potential and avoid promises before calibration.

## How Managers Are Evaluated

Managers are evaluated on team outcomes, employee growth, operating health, hiring quality, retention of strong performers, clarity during change, and willingness to address hard issues. A team that succeeds only through confusion, burnout, or hidden heroics is not being managed well.

Manager evaluation also considers the employee experience. Do reports know what is expected? Do they receive feedback in time to use it? Are promotions and reviews handled fairly? Are underperformance issues addressed early and humanely? Does the team make good decisions when the manager is not present? Are customers, systems, and sensitive data protected?

Good managers create leverage. Poor managers create dependency. If every decision waits for the manager, if every customer escalation requires the manager's personal rescue, or if every project needs the manager to remember hidden context, the system is fragile. The manager may be working hard, but the team is not being managed well.

## Failure Modes

Common manager failures at NovaOps include: skipping 1:1s, treating Slack responsiveness as performance, avoiding direct feedback, approving access informally, confusing friendship with fairness, rewarding only visible heroics, waiting too long to involve People Operations, and letting remote employees discover decisions after the fact.

Another failure mode is over-correcting into bureaucracy. A manager who makes every decision require a form, a meeting, and three approvals will slow the team unnecessarily. The standard is not maximum process. The standard is the right amount of clarity, review, and accountability for the risk.

When you are unsure, ask for help early. A manager who asks People Operations, IT, Finance, or a peer manager for guidance before acting is usually protecting the team. A manager who improvises privately on sensitive issues is usually creating cleanup work for someone else.
