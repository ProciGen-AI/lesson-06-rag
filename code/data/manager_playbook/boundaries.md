# Boundaries

## Why Boundaries Matter

Managers at NovaOps care about people as humans, but the manager role has limits. You are not a therapist, lawyer, doctor, immigration adviser, payroll specialist, security analyst, or best friend in a reporting relationship. You are accountable for creating a healthy work environment and connecting employees to the right help.

Good boundaries make trust stronger. They reduce favoritism, protect confidentiality, and prevent managers from making promises the company cannot keep.

Boundaries are especially important at NovaOps because the company is remote-first and hybrid across Israel, Germany, the United Kingdom, the United States, and contractor locations. A manager may be the person an employee talks to most often, but that does not make the manager the owner of every personal, legal, payroll, security, or medical issue. The manager's job is to listen respectfully, separate work impact from private facts, and route the issue to the right owner.

Good boundaries are not cold. They are clear. A manager can say, "I care about what you are dealing with, and I want to make sure the right people help. I can talk with you about schedule and coverage, and People Operations should handle the leave details." That sentence protects the employee better than informal advice, secret side agreements, or promises made in the moment.

## Confidentiality

Treat employee information as confidential by default. Share it only with people who need it for a legitimate business or legal reason. Performance concerns may be shared with People Operations and your manager. Medical details should go to People Operations, not into your personal notes or Slack messages. Payroll and benefits questions should be routed through BambooHR or HR.

Do not promise absolute secrecy. If an employee raises harassment, discrimination, safety, security, self-harm, fraud, or serious policy concerns, you may need to escalate. Say that plainly and kindly.

Confidential employee information includes compensation, leave status, medical or family details, immigration or work authorization questions, performance reviews, correction plans, investigation notes, harassment or discrimination reports, payroll issues, benefits elections, home addresses, personal phone numbers, government IDs, and access incidents tied to a named person. Treat that information as need-to-know even if the employee seems comfortable discussing it casually.

Need-to-know is narrower than curiosity. It can include People Operations, your manager, the relevant department leader, IT for access or security work, Finance for spend or payroll-adjacent questions, and Legal when People Operations or leadership decides legal review is needed. It does not include peer managers who want context, teammates who ask what is going on, or friends at the company.

Do not use Slack, Webex chat, private Notion pages, personal documents, or screenshots as shadow HR systems. Formal HR records belong in BambooHR or in the process People Operations gives you. Sensitive work facts may belong in Jira, Salesforce, GitHub, or another operational system only when the fact is actually about the work and does not expose private employee details.

If an employee asks you to keep something secret, respond with a boundary before they share more: "I can keep this as private as possible, but I cannot promise absolute secrecy if it involves safety, harassment, discrimination, security, fraud, legal risk, or another serious policy concern." If the employee continues, listen, write down only the facts needed for escalation, and contact People Operations or the appropriate owner.

If you accidentally receive information you should not have, do not forward it around looking for advice. For example, if you are mistakenly added to a BambooHR thread with medical documentation, tell People Operations, delete local copies if instructed, and do not summarize the medical content in your own notes. If you see unexpected system access, contact IT rather than exploring what you can view.

When communicating to a team, share work coverage only. "Rachel is out this week; Yael will cover the renewal calls" is usually enough. Do not explain that someone is on medical leave, in a family crisis, under investigation, on a correction plan, or negotiating compensation unless People Operations has explicitly approved the wording.

## Medical, Leave, And Personal Privacy

Managers may discuss work coverage, timing, availability, transition plans, and the support needed to keep work moving. Managers should not ask for diagnoses, medications, treatment plans, family medical histories, doctor's notes, pregnancy details, mental health details, immigration paperwork, or the private reason for a protected leave request. If a document is required, People Operations should request and store it.

If an employee volunteers medical or family information, do not probe. A good response is: "Thank you for telling me. I do not need the medical details, and I want People Operations to help with the leave or accommodation process. Let's talk about what coverage you need at work." In your notes, write the work-relevant fact, such as "employee asked about medical leave and temporary schedule coverage; referred to People Operations." Do not record the diagnosis or family story.

Leave and accommodation questions belong with People Operations. Sara Ben-David owns People Operations policy, Sara Cohen supports HR operations and contractor paperwork, and Maya Rosen coordinates HR workflows in the United States. A manager may confirm that the team can cover a certain period, identify critical handoffs, or adjust meeting load during a return, but People Operations confirms eligibility, documentation, legal requirements, and official leave status.

Do not treat approved leave, protected leave inquiries, medical accommodations, parental leave, caregiving responsibilities, or a return-from-leave ramp as performance problems. If performance concerns already exist and the employee raises leave or a medical issue, pause and involve People Operations before continuing. The concern may still need to be managed, but it must be handled through the right process.

For return-from-leave planning, ask about work preferences and constraints rather than personal details. Appropriate questions include: "What schedule constraints should we plan around?", "Which meetings should we prioritize in the first two weeks?", "What changed while you were away that would help to review?", and "Do you want a written digest or a Webex walkthrough?" Inappropriate questions include asking what treatment they received, why a family member needs care, or whether the employee is fully recovered.

If a teammate asks why another employee is absent, use neutral language. Say that the person is away or unavailable and name the coverage plan. Do not invite speculation. Do not say "medical leave," "mental health leave," "family issue," "accommodation," "burnout," or "HR situation" unless People Operations has approved that exact message.

## Compensation, Immigration, Legal, And Payroll Limits

Managers must not promise compensation changes, promotion outcomes, bonus treatment, severance, final pay timing, benefits continuation, tax treatment, contractor classification, visa outcomes, work authorization, relocation approval, or legal interpretations. You can explain the process and the evidence you will provide. You cannot guarantee the result.

Compensation and promotion decisions require the defined process. Promotion cycles, calibration, People Operations review, department leadership review, and Finance review exist so similar evidence is treated similarly. A manager should not soften disappointment by saying "you will definitely get promoted next cycle" or "I will make sure Finance approves the raise." A better boundary is: "I will document the evidence and sponsor the case if it is ready. The final decision goes through calibration and Finance review."

Immigration and cross-border work questions require People Operations. Do not tell an employee that they can work from another country for a month, move payroll locations, start work before authorization is complete, or keep working after a visa or work authorization concern arises. Remote-first does not mean work-from-anywhere without review. People Operations may need to check employment status, payroll setup, local law, benefits, equipment shipping, data access, tax, and security constraints.

Legal questions should be routed through People Operations or leadership. Managers should not interpret employment agreements, contractor statements of work, confidentiality agreements, customer contract terms, non-solicitation language, local employment law, severance rights, data privacy requirements, or investigation rules. If an employee asks, "Is this legal?" the manager should say that the company will route the question to the right owner, not improvise an answer.

Payroll and benefits questions should be routed through BambooHR or HR. Finance may be involved for payroll blockers, expense treatment, spend approvals, or budget impact, but managers should not calculate payroll, tax, benefits, severance, or final pay themselves. Tom Baker in Finance reviews spend thresholds and finance-owned approvals; People Operations owns HR process and employee records.

## Time And Availability

Remote-first work can blur hours. Managers should model sustainable boundaries: use scheduled messages when writing late, avoid praising weekend work as the default, and make urgent channels explicit. If a production incident, customer escalation, or payroll blocker is truly urgent, use the escalation path. Otherwise, let people work in their local day.

You may be friendly with reports, but do not build a social pattern that makes access to you unequal. Important context, feedback, and opportunities should not depend on who happens to chat with you after hours.

Every team should have a written working agreement for local hours, expected response times, urgent channels, handoff rituals, and on-call or escalation ownership. A manager in the United Kingdom should not assume a report in Israel, Germany, or the United States is available just because Slack is open. A manager in the United States should not routinely push decision meetings into late evenings for colleagues in Israel or Europe. Rotate inconvenient times when a meeting genuinely needs everyone.

Use scheduled send for nonurgent late-night or weekend messages. If you must write after hours, make the expectation explicit: "No reply needed until your workday." Do not reward constant availability as a sign of commitment. If someone repeatedly works weekends or late nights, thank them for the effort and then inspect capacity, staffing, priorities, or incident load.

Urgent should mean urgent. Production incidents, active customer escalations, safety or security concerns, and payroll blockers may justify after-hours escalation. A normal Jira question, a promotion draft, a nonblocking Salesforce update, a routine 1:1 topic, or a manager's preference for a fast answer does not. If a topic is urgent, name the impact, deadline, owner, and next action rather than sending a vague "ping."

Use Webex or an in-person meeting for sensitive conversations that need nuance: performance risk, conflict, leave return, accommodations, role changes, compensation disappointment, or termination. Slack is useful for lightweight coordination, but it is not the right place to deliver difficult feedback or discuss private employee details.

Do not create hidden work channels that exclude people. If a decision happens in an after-hours chat, a social thread, or a private message, move the decision into the normal team system so the whole team has equal access. For Engineering, that may be Jira, GitHub, or an architecture note. For Sales and Customer Success, that may be Salesforce, a customer note, or a team update. For HR, IT, and Finance, use the approved workflow.

## Friendship, Social Boundaries, And Favoritism

Managers can be warm, friendly, and human with reports. They should not become best friends, private confidants, dating partners, financial helpers, or gatekeepers of special access. The reporting relationship creates power, even when both people like each other and the team culture is casual.

Favoritism can be real even when the manager's intent is good. Do not give the best projects, fastest feedback, travel opportunities, conference seats, schedule flexibility, public recognition, promotion coaching, or informal context only to the people who socialize with you most. If an opportunity matters, share the criteria and make the access visible.

Examples of risky patterns:

- A manager regularly gets dinner with one report and discusses upcoming team changes there before telling the team.
- A manager gives a friend the first chance at a high-visibility customer escalation because they happened to be chatting after hours.
- A manager reviews one person's promotion packet over the weekend as a favor but tells others to wait for the normal cycle.
- A manager approves flexible hours for a close friend but challenges another report for the same request.
- A manager lets a favored contractor keep broad Slack or GitHub access after the statement of work no longer requires it.
- A manager shares private frustration about one employee with another employee who is also a friend.

If you have a pre-existing friendship, family relationship, romantic history, financial relationship, or other conflict with a report, tell your manager and People Operations. The right answer may be a reporting-line change, an adjusted reviewer, another manager in calibration, or a documented conflict-management plan. Do not wait until promotion, compensation, performance, or termination decisions are already underway.

Do not borrow money from reports, lend money to reports, ask reports to support your side project, sell personal goods or services to reports, or pressure reports into social events, political causes, religious activities, investments, or personal favors. A request can feel optional to you and mandatory to the employee.

Social events should have a work purpose and a reasonable path for people who do not drink alcohol, travel easily, attend after hours, or share the majority culture. Remote teammates should not become second-class team members because the manager's best context happens at an office lunch, a local happy hour, or a private group chat.

## Decision Boundaries

Do not approve access, expenses, leave, compensation, promotion, or termination outside the defined process. Manager approval confirms business need; IT, Finance, or People Operations may still need to approve risk, cost, or employment impact.

If you are unsure where your authority ends, ask before acting. A slow correct answer is better than a fast unauthorized promise.

Managers usually have authority to set day-to-day priorities, assign work within a role, run 1:1s, give feedback, approve ordinary scheduling within team norms, clarify expectations, decide team coverage, and recommend recognition, promotion, hiring, or performance steps. That authority is real, but it does not override company process.

Manager approval means "this is needed for the work." It does not mean "the company has approved every risk." IT still decides whether an access pattern is safe. Finance still decides whether spend is within approval thresholds. People Operations still decides HR process, leave administration, employee records, and fairness checks.

Access decisions: Managers may request access and confirm business need. IT owns provisioning, security review, privileged access, MFA, device management, and access removal. AWS admin, GitHub organization admin, Okta admin, Salesforce admin, and BambooHR admin access require extra review. Contractor access, including GitHub access for Priya Raman, Alex Kim, or any contractor, requires manager and IT approval with a defined end date.

Spend decisions: Managers should involve Finance before approving new paid tools, license expansions, equipment above USD 1,500, or recurring spend above USD 500 per month or USD 2,500 per year. A tool may be useful and still need Finance review. Use [billing review](https://billing.novaops.example) when a request may create new paid spend.

Leave decisions: Managers may discuss coverage and timing, but People Operations owns eligibility, documentation, official leave status, and accommodation process. Do not create private leave arrangements that conflict with company records. Use [BambooHR](https://bamboohr.novaops.example) or the workflow People Operations provides.

Performance decisions: Managers own expectations, feedback, evidence, and the performance conversation. People Operations owns process quality and fairness for formal steps. Involve People Operations before calling something a formal warning, correction plan, performance plan, final warning, job-in-jeopardy situation, or termination.

Promotion and compensation decisions: Managers may sponsor a case and provide evidence. Promotion is not final until calibration and required People Operations and Finance review are complete. Compensation changes are not final until the approved process is complete. Do not promise a title, salary, effective date, bonus, or exception before approval.

Termination decisions: Managers do not decide or communicate termination terms alone. People Operations must be involved before a final decision or final conversation. The manager may schedule and deliver the approved conversation, but People Operations prepares materials and follows up on paperwork, benefits, severance if applicable, and equipment return. IT handles access removal.

## Escalation Paths

Escalate early when the issue has employment, safety, security, financial, or fairness impact. Early escalation is not a failure of management. It is how managers keep trust with employees and avoid inventing process under pressure.

## Remote-First Edge Cases

Remote-first work requires more explicit boundaries than office-only work. Local hours, local holidays, caregiving schedules, employment location, payroll setup, equipment shipping, and data access can all affect what a manager may approve.

Do not approve a temporary or permanent work-location change without People Operations review. A report may ask to work from another country for family reasons, extend travel, move to a new city, or start work while visiting a location where NovaOps does not normally employ people. The manager can describe business coverage needs, but People Operations must check employment, payroll, immigration, benefits, and local-law implications. IT may also need to review device and data-access risk.

Do not ask employees to post home addresses, personal phone numbers, passport details, medical documents, or family details in Slack. For equipment shipping, payroll, benefits, and leave records, use BambooHR, the IT request portal, or the process People Operations gives you.

Be careful with meeting recordings and transcripts. Do not record sensitive conversations about performance risk, leave, accommodations, compensation, investigation intake, or termination unless People Operations has approved the approach and everyone who must consent has consented. A transcript can expose more private information than the conversation required.

For remote incidents, separate urgency from convenience. If an engineer is on call for a production incident, use the incident path. If a Customer Success escalation is active and a customer meeting is at risk, use the agreed customer escalation path. If the issue can wait until the local workday, let it wait. A remote-first culture should not mean every home becomes an always-open office.

Remote social life needs fairness too. Do not make office presence, local happy hours, or informal after-hours chat the route to better context. Publish decisions. Repeat important context in the normal system. Invite input asynchronously when time zones make live attendance unreasonable.

## Practical Scripts

When an employee shares medical details: "I am sorry you are dealing with that. I do not need the medical details, and I want to protect your privacy. People Operations should help with leave or accommodation questions. I can work with you on coverage and schedule."

When an employee asks for secrecy: "I will keep this as private as I can, but I cannot promise absolute secrecy if it involves safety, harassment, discrimination, security, fraud, legal risk, or serious policy concerns. If that is part of it, I may need to involve the right owner."

When an employee asks for a raise promise: "I can discuss your impact and the process, and I can tell you what evidence would support a compensation or promotion case. I cannot promise an outcome before calibration and Finance review."

When an employee asks to work from another country: "I can look at team coverage, but I cannot approve cross-border work. People Operations needs to review employment, payroll, immigration, benefits, and local requirements before we answer."

When a report wants urgent access: "I can confirm whether the access is needed for the work. IT still needs to review risk and provision it. If the access creates new spend, Finance may also need to approve it."

When a teammate asks why someone is gone: "They are away right now. Coverage is handled by [owner], and we will update the team if anything changes." Do not add private reasons.

When you made a boundary mistake: "I may have taken this outside the right process. I am going to pause, involve the right owner, and make sure we handle it correctly from here." Then do that promptly.
